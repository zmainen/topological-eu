About TTT

A relational, pluralistic ontology — from phenomenology to data structures.

The Topological Theory of Things (TTT) is a philosophical program that develops a relational situational ontology: a formal account of reality in which things are constituted by their relations, not by hidden essences or intrinsic properties. It is flat — no viewpoint is privileged. It is pluralistic — the same world admits multiple valid descriptions at different scales. And it is formally precise — the core is machine-checked in Lean 4.

TTT grows from a specific philosophical lineage. It takes seriously the speculative realist commitment to a reality independent of human access, while rejecting the essentialism of object-oriented ontology (OOO). Where Graham Harman posits withdrawn objects with hidden real qualities, TTT shows that the surplus — the fact that things always exceed any particular encounter — follows directly from relational depth, without invoking hidden natures. Withdrawal is the shadow cast by partial activation over a relational field, not evidence of a non-relational core.

The philosophical ancestry runs through the Recognition school of Kashmir Shaivism (consciousness as the universe's self-unfolding), Leibniz's relational monads, William James's radical empiricism and pluralism, Whitehead's process philosophy, and Badiou's set-theoretic ontology. From Kashmir Shaivism, that recognition — not discovery — is the fundamental epistemological act; from Leibniz, that perspective is constitutive; from James, that reality always exceeds every determination; from Whitehead, that process precedes substance; from Badiou, that the undifferentiated ground precedes every act of differentiation.

The core idea

An ontology is a tool for thinking — a way of carving reality into what can be named, related, and reasoned about. Every ontology makes a wager about what is fundamental. Harman wagers on objects: real and sensual, each with real and sensual qualities, yielding a fourfold structure and sixteen possible tensions. Substance metaphysics wagers on essences — hidden natures that appearances only partially reveal. TTT wagers on relations. There is nothing beneath or behind them. What a thing is is constituted by what it connects to, what position it occupies in a web of other things. There is no residual thingness left over when you subtract the relations.

This is a pluralistic commitment. If things are constituted by their relations, then the same thing encountered in different situations — by different observers, at different scales, in different contexts — is genuinely different each time, because each encounter activates a different subset of its relational character. And yet it is also the same thing: not because of a hidden essence carrying it forward, but because a pattern of relational invariance persists through the variation. TTT calls this pattern topology — the structure that is preserved when everything else changes.

Identity, on this account, is topological. The marble worn smooth is still the marble; the person who has aged is still the person. Not because of a hidden soul, but because the connectivity — the relational pattern — holds through the transformation. When the pattern breaks, identity breaks. The topology/geometry distinction — invariant structure versus variable instantiation — does the work that essences were supposed to do, without the metaphysical baggage. And because no single encounter exhausts a thing's relations, every perspective is partial but real. The world is perspectival and multi-scale without collapsing into relativism: there is a fact about every relation, even though no single viewpoint captures all of them.

From philosophy to data structures

TTT is not only a philosophical argument. The single-relation ontology is implemented as a working formal system. The companion paper The Library Theorem (draft, 2026) proves that organized external structure governs agentic reasoning capacity — that how you organize knowledge is not incidental but constitutive of what you can think. The belongs-to formalism is verified in Lean 4: all twelve reductions from standard ontological relations to qualified belongs-to are machine-checked.

This formal precision makes TTT applicable where philosophy alone cannot reach. The same ontology that addresses the problem of identity in metaphysics serves as the schema for a working knowledge system. Entities, situations, qualities, and the belongs-to relation map directly onto data structures — graphs, tables, indices — that can represent scientific data, institutional knowledge, and world models at arbitrary scale.

TTT is now being applied to the design of ontologies for general world models: representations of reality sufficient to support reasoning and action by artificial agents. The argument is that the right ground for such models is neither propositional (lists of facts about intrinsic properties) nor essentialist (hidden natures behind appearances) but purely relational. A world model built on belongs-to can represent mathematics as easily as social arrangements, can model the same world at different scales, and carries its own perspectival structure without collapsing into relativism.

Philosophical context

Key external references that situate TTT within the broader conversation.

  • Kshemarāja, Pratyabhijnahrdayam (c. 1000 CE) The Recognition Sutras of Kashmir Shaivism. Consciousness as the universe's self-unfolding — neither monist nor dualist. The tradition that independently arrived at the same ontological structure as TTT, from the opposite direction. Via Christopher Wallis's translation.
  • Leibniz, Monadology (1714) Each monad is a perspective on the relational whole. Identity through perspective, not substance. The ancestor who almost got there.
  • James, A Pluralistic Universe (1909) "Ever not quite." Reality exceeds every determination. Radical empiricism and conjunctive relations.
  • Whitehead, Process and Reality (1929) Process precedes substance. Actual occasions are constituted by their relations.
  • Rosen, Life Itself (1991) A living system is simultaneously its own model. The modeler cannot be excluded from the model.
  • Barfield, Saving the Appearances (1957) Participation and disenchantment. What was lost when the world became a collection of objects viewed from outside.
  • Badiou, Being and Event (1988) Ontology is mathematics. Inconsistent multiplicity precedes every structuring operation.
  • Harman, The Quadruple Object (2011) Object-oriented ontology. TTT's primary interlocutor — accepts the democratization of ontology, rejects the withdrawn essence.
  • DeLanda, Intensive Science and Virtual Philosophy (2002) Flat ontology, topology as the basis of individuation. A parallel program with different formal commitments.
  • Gibson, The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception (1979) Affordances cut across the subjective–objective dichotomy. Perception as relational, not representational.
