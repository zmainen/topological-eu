The core idea

An ontology is a tool for thinking — a way of carving reality into what can be named, related, and reasoned about. Every ontology makes a wager about what is fundamental. Substance metaphysics wagers on essences. Object-oriented ontology wagers on withdrawn objects. TTT wagers on relations. There is nothing beneath or behind them. What a thing is is constituted by what it connects to, what position it occupies in a web of other things.

This is a pluralistic commitment. The same thing encountered in different situations is genuinely different each time, because each encounter activates a different subset of its relational character. And yet it is also the same thing — not because of a hidden essence, but because a pattern of relational invariance persists through the variation. TTT calls this pattern topology: the structure preserved when everything else changes.

The world is perspectival and multi-scale without collapsing into relativism: there is a fact about every relation, even though no single viewpoint captures all of them.

  • Leibniz, Monadology (1714) —Identity through perspective, not substance.
  • James, A Pluralistic Universe (1909) —Reality exceeds every determination. Radical empiricism.
  • Barfield, Saving the Appearances (1957) —Participation and disenchantment.
  • Rosen, Life Itself (1991) —The modeler cannot be excluded from the model.
  • Harman, The Quadruple Object (2011) —Primary interlocutor. TTT accepts the democratization, rejects the withdrawn essence.
  • DeLanda, Intensive Science and Virtual Philosophy (2002) —Flat ontology, topology as individuation.
